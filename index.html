Australia in the age of AI

Who's using it, who's benefiting,
and who's being left behind.

Nearly half of all Australians now use generative AI, and over half of businesses have adopted it in some form. But beneath these numbers, a fracture is forming, between cities and regions, large enterprises and small ones, and workers whose skills are suddenly in demand and those whose jobs face an uncertain future.

This is the story of that divide.

Chapter 2 The Business Divide

AI skill demand has grown fourfold in a decade

In 2015, fewer than 1 in 500 Australian job postings required AI-related skills. By 2021 that had risen to 1 in 100, a fivefold increase in six years. Even after a slight pullback, demand in 2024 remains four times its 2015 level.

2021 was the peak, the year AI became a genuine hiring requirement, not just a niche skill for tech specialists.

Some industries are racing. Others don't know the race has started.

Services and retail lead AI adoption, but the more revealing story is in awareness. Agriculture and distribution have the largest shares of businesses that simply don't know how to use AI, a fundamentally different barrier than choosing not to.

Manufacturing knows about AI but isn't using it. Agriculture doesn't know it's an option. These are two very different problems requiring two very different solutions.

Where AI companies exist is not where AI hiring happens

IT infrastructure dominates both, 346 companies and 4.2% of all sector job postings require AI skills. Business processes has the most companies of any sector, yet generates only moderate hiring demand. Media and healthcare have hundreds of AI companies but barely register on job demand.

IT infrastructure is the only sector with both scale and demand. Everywhere else, company count and hiring intensity tell two very different stories.

Chapter 3 The Skills Divide

Exposure

AI is reshaping some jobs far more than others

Arts and media professionals lead all occupations at 7.6%, more than eight times the national average of 0.9%. ICT professionals follow at 5.1%. Below the average line, health workers, hospitality staff, and drivers sit at just 0.1%, barely touched by AI hiring demand.

The gap between the most and least exposed occupations is 75-fold. This is not a gradual spectrum, it is a sharp divide.

Scale

The largest workforces aren't the most exposed

Each dot's size reflects how many Australians work in that occupation. Health professionals and hospitality workers are among the largest workforces, yet their AI exposure is near zero. Education professionals, with over a million workers, sit mid-chart at 2.8%: large, and increasingly in the firing line.

Arts and media is highly exposed but employs relatively few. The real risk is where large workforces meet rising demand, and Education is the clearest example.

Compounding divides

Place and industry stack against you

The divide is not just geographic, and not just sectoral, it compounds. Being in a low-adoption state and a low-adoption industry puts workers at a double disadvantage. The ACT's services sector sits at the hottest corner; the NT's agriculture and manufacturing at the coldest.

The gap between the hottest and coldest cell spans more than 60 percentage points. Where you live and what industry you work in together determine your exposure to the AI economy.

Chapter 4 The Future

The human scale

1 in 2 Australian businesses have made the leap

Each square is one percent of Australian businesses. Red squares have adopted AI in some form, grey squares have not. Just over half have crossed the line. But that average hides the fractures already documented: large businesses are at near 100%, while small businesses sit at 63%.

Each square = 1% of Australian businesses

Where are you in this grid? If you work in a small business outside a major city, the odds are you are still in the grey.

Where we're heading

The divide doesn't disappear, it shifts

Click a state to compare its 2024 adoption rate against its illustrative 2028 projection. Mid-tier states like Queensland and WA grow fastest in absolute terms. But Tasmania and the NT, already lowest, are projected to gain the least, leaving the geographic divide intact. The map gets warmer, but unevenly.

The ACT reaches an estimated 82% by 2028, the NT just 52%. A 30-point gap that structural barriers will not close on their own.

Source: NAIC AI Adoption Tracker, Q4 2024 · ⚑ 2028 figures are illustrative projections based on observed growth differentials, not forecasts. See methodology note.

Who pulls ahead

Mid-tier states surge. The bottom stays stuck.

Each line is a state. Its slope shows how fast AI adoption is projected to grow between 2024 and 2028. Queensland, Western Australia and South Australia show the steepest rises, starting from a lower base with more room to grow. Tasmania and the Northern Territory show the flattest lines, structural barriers limit growth regardless of headroom.

The steepest lines are not the highest ones. The divide doesn't close, it reorganises. Mid-tier states surge past where they were, but the bottom is left further behind in relative terms.
